Overview

Start with the context a new reader needs. Describe the project, its purpose, and why it mattered in two or three clear sentences.

Include: the main project goals, your contribution, your discipline, and a clear definition of success.

Goals

  • State the most important project goal.
  • Describe what success needed to look like.
  • Include a practical or user-focused objective.

My contribution

Explain exactly what you owned, what you collaborated on, and how your discipline contributed to the project.

Process

Share the moments that changed the work, rather than trying to document every task.

Include: the evidence you gathered, alternatives you explored, decisions you made, feedback you received, and how the work improved.

Research

Explain what you needed to understand first and which findings shaped the direction of the project.

Show: research notes, precedents, site analysis, user needs, requirements, or source data.

Design

Present the early ideas you explored and explain why one direction became more promising than the others.

Show: sketches, options, diagrams, concepts, moodboards, or design principles.

Development

Describe how the selected idea became more resolved, practical, coordinated, and technically credible.

Show: drawings, models, prototypes, calculations, specifications, or coordination work.

Testing

Share the checks, feedback, simulations, reviews, or observations used to evaluate the developing work.

Show: test results, review comments, comparisons, validation criteria, or user feedback.

Refinement

Explain what changed after testing and how those final decisions improved clarity, usability, or performance.

Show: before-and-after comparisons, final adjustments, resolved details, or lessons applied.

The challenge

Write the central problem clearly from the user, client, or project point of view.

Include: evidence that the problem exists, who it affects, why it matters, and the constraints or competing priorities that made it difficult.

Problem #1 — Problem name

Explain the main gap, risk, inefficiency, unmet need, or opportunity in clear and specific terms.

Evidence: Add the observation, data, feedback, or condition that proves this problem exists.

Problem #2 — Problem name

Explain the second issue and how it compounds, influences, or conflicts with the first problem.

Consequence: State what happens if this problem is not addressed.

Key insight

Highlight the research finding or realization that changed the direction of the work.

Lead with the clearest result.

Show the thinking, not only the final image.

Outcome and impact

Describe the final result and connect it back to the original goals. Use measured outcomes where they are available and be clear when results are qualitative.

Final outcome: Explain what you produced and how the completed work addressed the original challenge.

Impact: Describe what improved for users, the client, the environment, project performance, or future work.

Evidence of success: Include any relevant measurements, testing, feedback, approval, comparison, or observed result. Mention limitations honestly when evidence is qualitative.

Reflection

Close the case study with an honest assessment of the work and what it changed in your practice.

Include: what worked, what you learned, what you would change, and the most valuable next step.

Success

What worked well

Identify a decision, method, collaboration, or result that made the project stronger.

Learning

What I learned

Share a specific lesson about the work, collaboration, technical process, or your own practice.

Next step

What I would do next

Describe the next test, improvement, or phase you would prioritise with more time.

Final takeaway

End with one concise sentence that captures why this project matters to you and what it demonstrates about your approach.
